## Today's Five Terms

### 1. Spot Price

**Plain-English definition:** The spot price is the current market price at which a commodity can be bought or sold for immediate delivery.

**Everyday analogy:** It is like the price tag on a product at a store — what you pay right now to take it home today.

**Market example:** If crude oil's spot price is $85 per barrel, a refinery buying oil today pays that price for barrels delivered immediately.

**Why it matters:** The spot price reflects real-time supply and demand conditions and is the benchmark from which futures prices are compared.

---

### 2. Futures Contract

**Plain-English definition:** A futures contract is a legal agreement to buy or sell a specific commodity at a predetermined price on a set date in the future.

**Everyday analogy:** It is like agreeing today to buy next month's grocery order at this week's prices, locking in the cost before prices change.

**Market example:** An airline might buy crude oil futures contracts to lock in fuel costs for the next six months, protecting itself against price spikes.

**Why it matters:** Futures contracts allow producers and consumers to hedge against price volatility and give traders a way to speculate on price direction.

---

### 3. Contango

**Plain-English definition:** Contango is a market condition in which the futures price of a commodity is higher than its current spot price.

**Everyday analogy:** Imagine paying a premium to reserve a hotel room six months in advance because the hotel expects to be fully booked and prices to rise.

**Market example:** When crude oil futures for delivery in three months trade at $88 while the spot price is $85, the market is in contango.

**Why it matters:** Contango signals that markets expect future prices to be higher, often reflecting storage costs or anticipated supply tightening.

---

### 4. Backwardation

**Plain-English definition:** Backwardation is a market condition in which the futures price of a commodity is lower than its current spot price.

**Everyday analogy:** It is like a store offering a discount for customers who pre-order next season's inventory, because demand is high right now but expected to cool.

**Market example:** If the spot price of natural gas is $3.50 per MMBtu but a three-month futures contract trades at $3.20, the market is in backwardation.

**Why it matters:** Backwardation often signals strong immediate demand or tight near-term supply, and traders pay close attention to shifts between contango and backwardation.

---

### 5. Inventory Report

**Plain-English definition:** An inventory report is a periodic government or industry release that shows how much of a commodity — such as crude oil or natural gas — is currently held in storage.

**Everyday analogy:** Think of it as a weekly count of how many items are sitting in a warehouse. If stocks are lower than expected, prices often rise; if higher, prices may fall.

**Market example:** The U.S. Energy Information Administration releases a weekly crude oil inventory report. A surprise drawdown often pushes oil prices higher within minutes.

**Why it matters:** Inventory levels are one of the most watched supply indicators in commodity markets. Unexpected results can cause sharp price moves in oil, gas, and related assets.

## Market Cause-and-Effect Chain

1. Major oil-producing nations announce unexpected production cuts.
2. Traders anticipate tighter future supply and increase purchases of futures contracts.
3. Futures prices rise above the current spot price, placing the market in contango.
4. Refiners and storage operators buy physical oil today and sell futures contracts simultaneously to lock in the price gap — a strategy called a cash-and-carry trade.
5. Increased storage demand draws down available inventory, further supporting higher spot prices.

**Correct answer:** Contango

**Explanation:** Contango occurs when futures prices exceed the spot price, typically reflecting storage costs and expectations of higher future prices.
